Hello,

    Good points. Thanks for your comments. Obviously I think you can
do everything in text - a great example is to learn from 20 years of
tex/latex typesetting.

    However, of course, there's no "magic" e.g. your index or table of
contents will NOT auto-build or auto-write itself out-of-the-blue.

   As a real-world example the manuscripts book format [1], for
example, uses a simple text file for the table of contents.  For the
Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde novella see contents.yml [2]:

########################
# Table of Contents

- title: Story of the Door
  path: 01.md
- title: Search for Mr. Hyde
  path: 02.md
- title: Dr. Jekyll was Quite at Ease
  path: 03.md
- title: The Carew Murder Case
  path: 04.md
- title: Incident of the Letter
  path: 05.md
- title: Remarkable Incident of Dr. Lanyon
  path: 06.md
- title: Incident at the Window
  path: 07.md
- title: The Last Night
  path: 08.md
- title: Dr. Lanyon's Narrative
  path: 09.md
- title: Henry Jekyll's Full Statement of the Case
  path: 10.md

  In simple cases you CAN auto-generate the table of contents but
usually I'd say nothing wrong with "hand-writing/coding" the table of
contents. The same goes for the index and so on.

  Cheers.

PS: You can do everything in text (markdown and friends) if anything
is missing just use an extension; if there's no extension than you're
lucky - make history - and invent the future. It's that easy.
